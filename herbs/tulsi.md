This is important. "Tulsi" in common Indian usage refers to multiple species, and there is persistent botanical confusion in both traditional and commercial literature.

## 1. Overview

Tulsi is among the most culturally significant and widely consumed herbs in India. It grows in the courtyard of an estimated 60-80% of Hindu households and is consumed daily as tea, fresh leaves, or in various preparations by millions of Indians.

### Classical References

- **Charaka Samhita** â€” Referenced as Surasa. Classified under Shwasahara (anti-asthmatic), Hikka Nigrahana (anti-hiccough), and Krimighna (anti-parasitic) groups. Used in formulations for Kasa (cough), Shwasa (dyspnoea), and Krimi (parasitic conditions).
- **Sushruta Samhita** â€” Mentioned for Karna Roga (ear conditions â€” oil drops), and as part of anti-venom formulations (Visa Chikitsa).
- **Bhavaprakasha Nighantu** â€” Detailed monograph under Pushpa Varga. Describes both Shweta (white/green â€” Rama) and Krishna (dark) varieties. Notes it as Deepana (digestive stimulant), Hridya (cardiotonic), and Krimighna.
- **Dhanvantari Nighantu** â€” Lists it among Karaveeradi Varga.
- **Raja Nighantu** â€” Describes Tulsi as Bahumanjari (many-flowered), notes its Katu (pungent) and Tikta (bitter) rasa.

### Traditional Indications (Ayurvedic)
- Kasa (Cough)
- Shwasa (Dyspnoea / asthmatic conditions)
- Pratishyaya (Common cold / rhinitis)
- Jwara (Fever)
- Krimi (Parasitic conditions)
- Agnimandya (Digestive weakness)
- Karna Roga (Ear conditions â€” oil)
- Visa (Poisoning â€” part of classical anti-venom formulations)
- Daurgandhya (Halitosis / bad breath)
- Kushtha (Skin conditions)

### Rasayana Status
Tulsi is not classified as a primary Rasayana (rejuvenative) in the same tier as Ashwagandha or Amalaki in the classical Charaka hierarchy. However, Bhavaprakasha and later texts attribute Ayushya (life-promoting) and Balya (strength-giving) properties to it. Its daily use in Indian culture is more aligned with Swasthavritta (preventive health maintenance) than formal Rasayana therapy.

### What This Entry Does Not Cover
This is a pharmacological and safety analysis. Tulsi holds deep cultural and religious significance in Hindu tradition, which is acknowledged in Section 9 but does not inform the safety, dosing, or evidence assessments in this entry. Cultural reverence and pharmacological reality are separate domains.

## 2. Ayurvedic Perspective

| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| **Rasa** (Taste) | Katu (Pungent), Tikta (Bitter) |
| **Guna** (Qualities) | Laghu (Light), Ruksha (Dry), Tikshna (Sharp/Penetrating) |
| **Virya** (Potency) | Ushna (Heating) |
| **Vipaka** (Post-digestive effect) | Katu (Pungent) |

### Dosha Action
- **Kapha:** Strongly pacifies. Katu rasa + Ushna virya + Tikshna guna + Ruksha guna â€” this is an ideal Kapha-reducing profile. Primary classical use in Kapha disorders (respiratory congestion, sluggish digestion).
- **Vata:** Mixed. Ushna virya pacifies Vata. But Ruksha guna + Laghu guna + Katu vipaka can aggravate Vata with chronic or excessive use. Net effect depends on dose, duration, and individual constitution.
- **Pitta:** Aggravates. Katu rasa + Ushna virya + Tikshna guna + Katu vipaka â€” this is a Pitta-provocative combination. Not the herb of choice for Pitta-dominant individuals or Pitta-aggravated conditions.

### Why Tulsi May Aggravate Pitta in Excess

Every property of Tulsi is thermogenic:
- Katu rasa generates heat at the taste level
- Ushna virya generates systemic heat
- Tikshna guna has a penetrating, sharp quality
- Katu vipaka generates heat at the post-digestive stage

In Pitta-dominant individuals or in hot seasons (Grishma/summer), this thermal load can manifest as:
- Increased acidity / acid reflux
- Skin flare-ups (acne, rashes, inflammation)
- Increased irritability
- Burning sensation in urination
- Excessive thirst

This is why classical Ayurveda uses Tulsi primarily in Kapha-Vata conditions and in cold/wet seasons, not as a year-round universal supplement for all constitutions.

### Why Chronic Dry Use May Aggravate Vata

Tulsi has three Vata-aggravating gunas: Laghu (Light), Ruksha (Dry), Tikshna (Sharp). When consumed chronically in dry form (churna, dried tea, capsules) without a balancing vehicle:
- Ruksha guna accumulates over weeks/months, increasing dryness in the body
- Laghu guna reduces tissue heaviness â€” beneficial short-term but depleting long-term
- Katu vipaka creates a post-digestive drying effect

This manifests as:
- Dry skin, dry stools, constipation (classic Vata symptoms)
- Joint stiffness or cracking
- Anxiety, restlessness, light sleep
- Weight loss in already-lean individuals

**Mitigation:** Classical practice addresses this by taking Tulsi with honey (Madhura + Snigdha), warm milk, or ghee â€” all of which counterbalance the Ruksha and Laghu gunas. Pure dried Tulsi capsules, consumed daily for months without a balancing vehicle, bypass this traditional safeguard.

## 3. Modern Pharmacology

### Key Compounds

#### Eugenol
- **Concentration:** 40-70% of essential oil (varies by chemotype and variety; Krishna Tulsi typically higher)
- **Actions:** Anti-inflammatory (COX-1 and COX-2 inhibition), analgesic, antimicrobial, antioxidant
- **Note:** Eugenol is also the primary active compound in clove oil. At high concentrations, it is hepatotoxic and cytotoxic. The amounts in Tulsi tea or fresh leaves are far below toxic thresholds; concentrated essential oil is a different matter entirely (see Risk Coding).

#### Ursolic Acid
- **Type:** Pentacyclic triterpenoid
- **Actions:** Anti-inflammatory (NF-kB inhibition), anti-hyperglycaemic (enhances insulin sensitivity, inhibits alpha-glucosidase), anti-lipidemic, potential anti-tumour activity (in-vitro and animal studies only)
- **Note:** Present in many plants (rosemary, apple peel). Not unique to Tulsi.

#### Rosmarinic Acid
- **Type:** Polyphenol (ester of caffeic acid)
- **Actions:** Antioxidant (potent free radical scavenger), anti-inflammatory (inhibits complement system, COX-2), anti-allergic (inhibits mast cell degranulation in-vitro)
- **Note:** Also found in rosemary, lemon balm, sage. Well-characterized antioxidant.

#### Other Notable Compounds
- **Ocimumosides A and B** â€” Adaptogenic activity in animal models (anti-stress, cortisol modulation)
- **Apigenin** â€” Flavonoid. Anxiolytic and mild sedative properties. Also found in chamomile.
- **Linalool** â€” Monoterpene. Anxiolytic, antimicrobial. Present in essential oil.
- **Beta-caryophyllene** â€” Sesquiterpene. Anti-inflammatory (CB2 receptor agonist). Present in essential oil.
- **Oleanolic acid** â€” Triterpenoid. Hepatoprotective in animal studies. Anti-hyperglycaemic.
- **Vitamin C** â€” Present in fresh leaves (moderate amounts; significantly less than Amla)
- **Iron, Calcium, Zinc** â€” Present in leaves (minor nutritional contribution)

### Mechanistic Actions

**Anti-inflammatory:**
Multiple pathways â€” COX-2 inhibition (eugenol), NF-kB suppression (ursolic acid), complement inhibition (rosmarinic acid), CB2 agonism (beta-caryophyllene). This is a genuine multi-target anti-inflammatory profile. However, most evidence for systemic anti-inflammatory effect in humans is from small trials. The compound-level mechanisms are well-characterised in-vitro; the clinical translation is still building.

**Hypoglycaemic:**
Ursolic acid and oleanolic acid inhibit alpha-glucosidase and enhance peripheral glucose uptake. Several small human trials show fasting glucose reduction of 10-20% in type 2 diabetic patients taking Tulsi leaf powder. Clinically relevant but not a substitute for antidiabetic medication.

**"Adaptogenic" Claims â€” Critical Assessment:**
Tulsi is widely marketed as an "adaptogen." The term implies normalisation of stress response via HPA axis modulation. The evidence:
- Animal studies (Ocimumosides A and B): Positive. Reduced corticosterone, normalised neurotransmitter levels in stressed rats.
- Human trials: Very few. One small RCT (2012, Saxena et al.) showed improvement in generalised stress symptoms. A few open-label trials with methodological limitations.
- **Honest assessment:** The adaptogenic claim for Tulsi is Level B-C. It is not in the same evidence tier as Ashwagandha (Level A for stress/cortisol). The animal pharmacology is plausible, but the human clinical base is thin. Marketing has far outpaced the evidence.

**Antimicrobial:**
Eugenol and essential oil fractions show broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity in-vitro against gram-positive bacteria, some gram-negative bacteria, and Candida species. Clinically relevant for topical/oral applications (mouthwash, wound wash). Systemic antimicrobial effect from oral Tulsi tea consumption is unproven â€” the in-vivo concentrations are unlikely to reach antimicrobial thresholds in blood/tissue.

**Immunomodulatory â€” The Sensitive Claim:**
This needs careful handling because "immune booster" is the single most over-marketed claim for Tulsi in India.
- In-vitro and animal data: Increased NK cell activity, enhanced phagocytosis, modulation of cytokine profiles (both Th1 and Th2). Real, measurable effects in controlled settings.
- Human data: Extremely limited. No large-scale RCTs demonstrating clinically meaningful immune enhancement (e.g., reduced infection rates, faster recovery from illness) in humans taking Tulsi.
- **What this means:** Tulsi has immunomodulatory compounds. Whether drinking Tulsi tea meaningfully "boosts your immunity" against colds, flu, or other infections in a real-world setting is unproven. The leap from in-vitro cytokine modulation to "immunity booster" is scientifically unjustified at current evidence levels.

| Claim | Evidence Level | Notes |
|-------|---------------|-------|

**Evidence Level Key:**
- **A** = Multiple human RCTs or meta-analysis
- **B** = Small clinical trials with positive results
- **C** = Animal studies, in-vitro, or observational data
- **D** = Traditional use only, no modern clinical validation

## 4. Safe Usage Ranges (Educational Only)

> **"This is general educational information, not a medical prescription. Individual response varies based on constitution, health status, season, and concurrent medications. Consult a qualified practitioner before therapeutic use."**

### General Adult Ranges

| Form | Typical Range | Notes |
|------|--------------|-------|
| **Fresh leaves** | 5-10 leaves/day | Traditional Indian home consumption. Chewed directly or added to tea. Low risk at this level. Seasonal appropriateness matters (see below). |
| **Dried leaf tea (infusion)** | 2-5 g dried leaves per cup, 1-2 cups/day | Steep in hot water for 5-10 minutes. Most common commercial form. |
| **Leaf powder (Churna)** | 1-3 g/day | Taken with honey, warm water, or milk. |
| **Standardized extract** (2.5% ursolic acid typical) | 300-600 mg/day | Extract capsules. More concentrated than tea. Used in most clinical trials. |
| **Seed (Tulsi beej)** | 1-2 g/day | Soaked in water. Traditional use as cooling drink (Tukmalanga in Unani). Different profile from leaves â€” mucilaginous, cooling. |
| **Essential oil â€” Topical** | 1-2 drops diluted in carrier oil | For skin/joint application only. Never apply undiluted. Patch test first. |
| **Essential oil â€” Internal** | **NOT RECOMMENDED** | See Risk Coding. Concentrated eugenol is hepatotoxic and cytotoxic at oral doses. Essential oil ingestion is not traditional Ayurvedic practice and carries significant risk. |

### Short-Term vs Long-Term Use

**Short-term (1-4 weeks):**
- Well-tolerated in most adults.
- Appropriate for acute Kapha conditions (colds, congestion, cough) â€” this is the classical primary use.
- Higher doses (up to 5g churna/day) acceptable short-term under practitioner guidance.

**Long-term continuous use (>3 months):**
- This is where nuance matters, because millions of Indians drink Tulsi tea daily year-round.
- **Considerations:**
  - Chronic Ruksha guna accumulation (see Ayurvedic Perspective) â€” may increase Vata over time, especially in Vata-dominant individuals or in dry/cold seasons.
  - Chronic eugenol exposure â€” at Tulsi tea levels, likely below toxicity thresholds, but long-term safety studies specifically on daily Tulsi tea consumption over years are absent.
  - Blood-thinning effect â€” mild antiplatelet properties become relevant over chronic use, especially if combined with other antiplatelet agents or before surgery.
  - Fertility question â€” animal data suggests anti-fertility effects at high doses (see Risk Coding). Whether chronic moderate-dose tea consumption affects human fertility is unknown, but the uncertainty itself warrants caution for those trying to conceive.
  - Blood sugar modulation â€” for non-diabetics, chronic consumption at normal tea doses is unlikely to cause hypoglycemia. For diabetics on medication, ongoing monitoring is warranted.

**Seasonal Consideration (Ritucharya):**
Classical Ayurveda would not prescribe the same herb year-round without seasonal adjustment:
- **Best season:** Varsha (monsoon) and Hemanta/Shishira (winter/late winter) â€” Kapha-predominant seasons where Tulsi's heating, drying, Kapha-clearing properties are most appropriate.
- **Caution in:** Grishma (summer) â€” excessive heat + Tulsi's Ushna virya = Pitta aggravation potential. Reduce dose or frequency, or combine with cooling agents.

### Liver Enzyme Considerations

- Eugenol is metabolised by the liver (CYP enzymes). At the concentrations present in Tulsi tea (1-2 cups/day), hepatic load is minimal.
- At concentrated extract doses or with essential oil ingestion, eugenol can cause hepatocellular damage. Animal studies demonstrate dose-dependent hepatotoxicity with pure eugenol.
- No widespread clinical reports of liver injury from standard Tulsi tea consumption. However, long-term safety monitoring data is absent.
- If using concentrated extracts long-term (>3 months), periodic liver function testing is a reasonable precaution â€” not because injury is expected, but because data to exclude it is insufficient.

## 6. Side Effects

### Common (dose-dependent, generally mild)
- **GI irritation** â€” Nausea, burning sensation in stomach, acid reflux. More common on empty stomach, at higher doses, or in Pitta-dominant individuals. Tikshna guna (sharp/penetrating quality) irritates gastric mucosa directly.
- **Increased body heat sensation** â€” Subjective warmth, increased thirst. Expected pharmacological effect of Ushna virya, not necessarily a "side effect" â€” but unpleasant in summer or for Pitta constitutions.
- **Mild blood sugar lowering** â€” At standard tea doses, usually subclinical. Becomes a side effect in diabetics on medication (hypoglycemia risk) or in individuals who are already hypoglycemia-prone.

### Uncommon
- **Headache** â€” Reported by some users, particularly with concentrated extracts. May relate to blood pressure changes or individual sensitivity to eugenol.
- **Skin dryness / dryness of mucous membranes** â€” With chronic daily use. Reflects Ruksha guna accumulation.
- **Increased bleeding tendency** â€” Mild. Usually not noticeable in healthy individuals. May manifest as prolonged bleeding from cuts, increased menstrual flow, or easy bruising. Becomes clinically relevant only with concurrent anticoagulants/antiplatelets or bleeding disorders.
- **Dizziness / lightheadedness** â€” Related to hypotensive or hypoglycemic effects. More common at higher doses or in predisposed individuals.
- **Staining of teeth** â€” With chronic strong Tulsi tea consumption. Cosmetic, not medical. Tannins and plant pigments.

### Rare
- **Allergic reaction** â€” Contact dermatitis (from handling plant), oral allergy symptoms, or systemic allergic response. Rare but documented. More likely in individuals allergic to other Lamiaceae family plants (mint, basil, rosemary, oregano).
- **Hepatic concern** â€” Theoretical with concentrated extracts or essential oil use. No widespread clinical reports from standard tea/leaf consumption. Animal data shows eugenol hepatotoxicity at high concentrations.
- **Fertility effects** â€” See Risk Coding section. Animal data only. Not confirmed in humans. Listed here because the signal exists and users should be informed.

### What About "Detox" Symptoms?
As with Triphala, some users report headaches, fatigue, or skin breakouts when starting Tulsi and attribute them to "detoxification." There is no evidence that Tulsi causes toxin mobilisation or elimination. These symptoms likely represent: GI irritation, blood sugar adjustment, Pitta aggravation (in Pitta-dominant individuals), or coincidental illness. If symptoms persist beyond a few days, reduce dose or discontinue.

## 8. Misuse Patterns in India

This section addresses real, widespread patterns. It is not intended to dismiss Tulsi's genuine pharmacological properties, but to draw a clear line between evidence-based use and culturally amplified overclaiming.

### Pattern 1: "Immunity Booster" Oversimplification

**What happens:** Tulsi is the single most marketed "immunity booster" herb in India. Every FMCG brand, every Ayurvedic company, every wellness influencer positions Tulsi as the first-line defence against illness. "Drink Tulsi tea daily for strong immunity" is treated as settled medical fact.

**The reality:** Tulsi contains immunomodulatory compounds (demonstrated in-vitro and in animal models). However:
- No large-scale human RCT has demonstrated that Tulsi consumption reduces infection rates, shortens illness duration, or meaningfully enhances immune defence in a clinically measurable way.
- "Immunomodulatory" does not mean "immunity booster." Modulation can mean up-regulation or down-regulation depending on context. The marketing only ever implies up.
- The gap between "this compound affects cytokines in a petri dish" and "drinking this tea prevents you from getting sick" is enormous. That gap is currently unfilled by human evidence.
- This does not mean Tulsi is useless for respiratory health â€” it has genuine mucolytic, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties that may help symptomatically. But "symptom relief in a cold" and "immune system booster" are very different claims.

### Pattern 2: COVID-Era Megadosing

**What happened:** During the COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2022), Tulsi consumption in India surged dramatically. Users consumed:
- 20-30+ fresh leaves daily (3-5x typical amounts)
- Multiple cups of concentrated Tulsi decoctions (Kadha)
- Tulsi extract capsules stacked with Giloy, Ashwagandha, and turmeric simultaneously
- Tulsi essential oil drops taken orally (dangerous)

**The problems:**
- No evidence that any of these practices prevented COVID-19 infection or reduced severity.
- Megadosing a Katu + Ushna + Tikshna herb caused widespread Pitta aggravation â€” acid reflux, gastric irritation, and skin issues were commonly reported but rarely attributed to the Kadha.
- Combined adaptogen stacking (Tulsi + Giloy + Ashwagandha + Haldi) without understanding individual Dosha profiles or drug interactions led to unpredictable polypharmacy effects.
- The AYUSH Ministry's promotion of specific Kadha recipes, while well-intentioned, was not backed by clinical trial evidence for COVID-19 specifically. This created an implied government endorsement that many users interpreted as proven efficacy.
- Some individuals with autoimmune conditions self-medicated with high-dose Tulsi for "immunity," potentially worsening their autoimmune dysregulation.

### Pattern 3: Essential Oil Ingestion Trend

**What happens:** Influenced by Western aromatherapy marketing and social media, some Indian users have begun ingesting Tulsi essential oil drops â€” either directly, in water, or in capsule form â€” believing it to be a concentrated form of Tulsi's benefits.

**The problem:** This is genuinely dangerous. Tulsi essential oil is 40-70% eugenol. Oral ingestion of concentrated eugenol:
- Is hepatotoxic at doses above 10-15 mg/kg body weight (animal data)
- Can cause gastric mucosal damage
- Can cause CNS depression at high doses
- Is not equivalent to drinking Tulsi tea (which contains trace amounts of eugenol in aqueous extraction, not concentrated volatile oil)

Traditional Ayurveda uses steam-distilled Tulsi oil topically and in Nasya (nasal drops) at tiny doses in specific formulations â€” NOT oral ingestion of undiluted or lightly diluted essential oil. This modern trend has no classical or clinical basis.

### Pattern 4: "Natural = Unlimited Safe"

**What happens:** Because Tulsi grows in every courtyard and is given to children by grandmothers, it carries an unearned halo of absolute safety. Users reason: "It's just a plant leaf from our garden â€” how can it be harmful?"

**The problem:** This logic does not apply to pharmacologically active plants. Tulsi contains eugenol (at high concentrations, a hepatotoxin), has antiplatelet activity, lowers blood sugar, and may affect fertility (animal data). "Natural" describes origin, not safety profile. Datura, Aconite, and Oleander are also natural. The dose, form, duration, and individual context determine safety â€” not the fact that it grows in your courtyard.

### Pattern 5: Combining Multiple "Immunity" Herbs Daily

**What happens:** Stacking Tulsi + Giloy + Ashwagandha + Turmeric + Neem daily, often in concentrated extract form, as a permanent "health regimen."

**The problem:**
- Each of these herbs has distinct pharmacological actions, Dosha effects, and drug interaction profiles
- Stacking them amplifies unpredictable interactions
- Giloy + Tulsi = compounded immunomodulatory and hypoglycaemic effects
- Ashwagandha + Tulsi = one is cooling (Ashwagandha's Madhura vipaka), the other is heating (Tulsi's Katu vipaka) â€” not inherently dangerous, but the combined effect depends on individual constitution, which most self-medicating users don't assess
- No clinical trial has studied these specific multi-herb daily stacks in combination
- Classical Ayurveda formulates multi-herb preparations with precise ratios for specific conditions and constitutions â€” not "take all of them, all the time, for general wellness"

## 9. Religious and Cultural Sensitivity

### Acknowledgement

Tulsi holds a position in Indian culture that no other medicinal plant occupies. It is:
- Revered as a manifestation of the goddess Lakshmi (Tulsi Devi) in Vaishnavism
- Present in the courtyard (Tulsi Vrindavan) of millions of Hindu households
- Integral to daily puja, marriage ceremonies (Tulsi Vivah), and Ekadashi observances
- Associated with spiritual purity, protection, and auspiciousness in Hindu tradition
- Considered sacred in its planting, maintenance, and even its harvest

This cultural reality is acknowledged and respected. The reverence that communities hold for Tulsi is their own, and this entry does not seek to diminish it.

### The Necessary Separation

However, this database operates on a different axis:

**Cultural reverence â‰  pharmacological safety.**

- The spiritual significance of Tulsi does not inform its drug interaction profile.
- The religious tradition of daily Tulsi consumption does not constitute clinical evidence for any health claim.
- Devotional practice is not subject to evidence grading; pharmacological claims are.
- A plant can be simultaneously sacred and pharmacologically active with contraindications. These are not contradictory â€” they are different categories of reality.

### Why This Matters for an AI Health Assistant

An AI health assistant serving Indian users will inevitably encounter questions where religious sentiment and health-seeking behaviour are intertwined:

- "Tulsi is sacred, how can it have side effects?"
- "My guru/pandit says Tulsi cures everything."
- "We've been giving Tulsi to the whole family daily for generations â€” are you saying that's wrong?"

The correct response is never to disrespect the cultural practice. It is also never to allow cultural sentiment to override pharmacological reality. The response should be:

*"Tulsi is a valued part of Indian tradition, and its traditional use in moderate amounts has a long history. This information is about specific situations â€” certain medical conditions, medications, or doses â€” where caution is needed. Cultural reverence and medical safety are both important, and they can coexist."*

## 10. When to See a Doctor

Seek professional medical advice if:

- **Persistent hypoglycemia symptoms** (shakiness, sweating, confusion, dizziness, palpitations) â€” Especially if diabetic and on medication. Stop Tulsi. Check blood glucose. Inform your physician. Medication adjustment may be needed.
- **Unusual bleeding or bruising** â€” Prolonged bleeding from minor cuts, spontaneous bruising, increased menstrual flow, blood in stools, blood in urine. Stop Tulsi and any other herbal supplements. Get a coagulation profile.
- **Severe allergic reaction** â€” Skin rash, hives, swelling (especially facial/throat), difficulty breathing, anaphylaxis. Stop Tulsi immediately. Seek emergency care if breathing is affected.
- **Persistent GI symptoms** â€” Acid reflux, burning, nausea, or abdominal pain that does not resolve within 3-5 days of stopping Tulsi. Could indicate underlying gastric pathology unrelated to Tulsi, or Tulsi-aggravated Pitta requiring assessment.
- **Pregnancy planning** â€” If you are trying to conceive (male or female) and have been consuming Tulsi regularly (especially concentrated extracts), discuss with your gynaecologist or fertility specialist. The animal fertility data, while unconfirmed in humans, warrants informed decision-making.
- **Thyroid function changes** â€” New symptoms of thyroid dysfunction (fatigue, weight change, temperature sensitivity, hair changes) that coincide with regular Tulsi use. Get thyroid function tests.
- **Liver symptoms** â€” Yellowing of skin/eyes, dark urine, persistent fatigue, upper right abdominal discomfort. Particularly relevant if using concentrated Tulsi extract or essential oil. Stop all Tulsi products. Seek care immediately.
- **Any new or worsening symptoms** that coincide with starting or increasing Tulsi consumption â€” Correlation is not causation, but it warrants professional evaluation.
- **Pre-surgery** â€” Inform your surgeon and anaesthesiologist about all herbal supplements including Tulsi. Discontinue at least 2 weeks before elective surgery.
